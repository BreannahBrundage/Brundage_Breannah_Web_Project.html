Exploring Utah National Parks

Utah is home to five beautiful national parks, each showcasing a variety of landscapes and geological formations of different kinds. Whether you are sight seeing or looking for an adventurous hike, Utah's national parks will give a unforgettable experience.

Zion National Park
The Zion National Park Narrows were carved out by the Virgin River over millions of years, the river once flowed 2,000 ft deep. Zion is the second most visited national park in the United States. Landscape includes large sandstone cliffs, mountains, and arches. Zion is also famous for its most dangerous trail, Angels Landing which can be the most scenic view in the entire park. Angels Landing must be reached by the West Rim Trail with dangerous drop offs, so it is best not to try this trail as a beginner. Don't worry Zion has several easy to moderate trails to check out for beginners or those afraid of heights. It is best to research about a trial beforehand to prepare, some trials can be moderate or hard due to the lengthy distance of the hike and not the elevation incline.
Bryce Canyon National Park
Bryce Canyon is known for its largest formation of hoodoos, which are calcium mudstones formed into various shapes such as slot canyons, and fins. These mudstones were formed by the erosion of frost and rain water deposits dissolving creating some of the most beautiful red and orange formations. Bryce Canyon is known for is maze like features providing a thrilling adventure, along the way you may spot the Utah prairie dogs; they can typically be spotted at higher elevation. You can visit the Navajo loop in the park for a short hike to find incredible views and find the amazing hoodoos.
Arches National Park
Arches National Park is most famous for its Delicate Arch which is one the Utah state sign. If you are looking for incredible views, Arches National Park will be extraordinary. The arches have been formed over millions of years of erosion from wind and water. These formations are changing everyday due to weathering conditions. Most people venture out to see the Delicate Arch trail, it is rated as a moderate hike and could be unsuitable for those with children, elders, or special needs. Alternatively, the Double Arch trail is 0.3 miles and provides a scenic view and the gorgeous arch structures. There are several more trails to check out before you go, but others go to Arches National Park to view the dark night sky. The national park offers a Certified Dark Sky park with little to no light pollution giving visitors incredible views of the night sky and at the right time the Milky Way!
Canyonlands National Park
Canyonlands National Park offers the most diverse terrain split into four different districts. The Island in the Sky is best know for breathtaking scenic views and surrounding canyons. The Needles is more backcountry with numerous spike formations, the backcountry is less accessible and needs more preparation. The Maze is also in the backcountry side and in total isolation so it is less visited by beginner hikers, its terrain can be quite treacherous. Lastly, the River district can be accessed through the Island in the Sky and offers a fun adventure rafting through the rapids of Canyonlands. If you prefer to stay on land and dry, you can access the riverside trails and experience the beauty of the River District.
Capitol Reef National Park
Capitol Reef is most known for its large cliffs and canyons, with a wide variety of trails for adventurous hikers. This National Park is also a Certified Dark Sky for visitors to take on extraordinary stargazing and astrology. This is the least visited park of the five, which can provide you with a more private and secluded adventure perfect for photography. Capitol Reef is home to the Petroglyphs Trail which is 0.6 miles round trip and gives visitors access to ancient rock art created by Native Americans thousands of years ago.
